Finance Knowledge Base for Vector Embeddings

Section: Core Investment Concepts
Investing means allocating capital today with the expectation of future return.
Saving prioritizes capital preservation and liquidity, while investing accepts risk for growth.
Risk is the uncertainty of outcomes; return is the gain or loss on an investment.
Expected return is the probability weighted average outcome.
Real return equals nominal return minus inflation.
Compounding is earning return on both principal and prior returns over time.
Time horizon is the expected holding period and strongly affects portfolio design.
Risk tolerance is an investor's psychological and financial ability to bear volatility and loss.
Risk capacity is the objective ability to take risk based on income stability, assets, and liabilities.
Diversification reduces idiosyncratic risk by combining imperfectly correlated assets.
Asset allocation is the long run mix among asset classes and is a major driver of outcomes.
Rebalancing restores target weights and controls drift in risk exposure.
Liquidity is the ability to convert an asset to cash quickly with minimal price impact.
Volatility is the dispersion of returns, often measured by standard deviation.
Drawdown is the decline from a portfolio peak to a trough.
Margin of safety is buying below estimated intrinsic value to reduce valuation risk.

Section: Asset Classes
Cash and cash equivalents include treasury bills and money market instruments.
Fixed income includes government, municipal, and corporate bonds.
Equities represent ownership claims on company cash flows and residual value.
Real estate includes direct property and listed vehicles such as REITs.
Commodities include energy, metals, and agriculture and may hedge inflation shocks.
Alternatives include private equity, private credit, hedge funds, infrastructure, and collectibles.
Cryptocurrencies and digital assets are high volatility speculative assets with evolving regulation.

Section: Equity Market Concepts
Common stock holders have voting rights and residual claims after creditors.
Preferred stock has fixed dividends and seniority over common equity.
Market capitalization equals share price multiplied by shares outstanding.
Large cap, mid cap, and small cap classify companies by market value.
Growth stocks are priced for high future earnings growth.
Value stocks trade at lower valuation multiples relative to fundamentals.
Dividend yield is annual dividend per share divided by current price.
Buybacks reduce share count and can increase earnings per share.
Earnings per share equals net income minus preferred dividends divided by diluted shares.
Price to earnings ratio compares price per share to earnings per share.
Price to book ratio compares market value to accounting equity.
Price to sales ratio compares market value to revenue.
Enterprise value equals equity value plus net debt and other claims.
EV/EBITDA is a capital structure neutral valuation multiple.
Free cash flow is operating cash flow minus capital expenditures.
Discounted cash flow valuation estimates present value of projected future cash flows.

Section: Bond and Fixed Income Concepts
A bond is a debt instrument with principal, coupon, and maturity.
Coupon rate is the annual interest percentage paid on face value.
Yield to maturity is the internal rate of return if held to maturity and coupons are reinvested.
Current yield equals annual coupon divided by market price.
Duration measures sensitivity of bond price to interest rate changes.
Convexity adjusts duration for non linear price response at larger rate moves.
Credit spread is the extra yield over a risk free benchmark for credit risk.
Investment grade debt has lower default risk than high yield debt.
Default risk is the possibility the issuer cannot meet debt obligations.
Sovereign bonds are issued by national governments.
Municipal bonds often offer tax advantages depending on jurisdiction.
Inflation linked bonds adjust principal or coupons with inflation indices.
Yield curve plots yields across maturities and signals growth and policy expectations.
An inverted yield curve can indicate tighter financial conditions and recession risk.

Section: Portfolio Theory and Risk Metrics
Modern portfolio theory shows portfolio risk depends on covariance, not only individual volatility.
Correlation ranges from minus one to plus one and measures co movement between assets.
Beta measures asset sensitivity to broad market moves.
Alpha is return above a risk adjusted benchmark expectation.
Sharpe ratio equals excess return over risk free rate divided by volatility.
Sortino ratio penalizes downside volatility rather than total volatility.
Information ratio measures active return divided by tracking error.
Tracking error is volatility of active return relative to benchmark.
Value at Risk estimates potential loss at a confidence level over a horizon.
Expected shortfall estimates average loss beyond the Value at Risk threshold.
Stress testing evaluates outcomes under adverse hypothetical scenarios.
Scenario analysis evaluates portfolio behavior under defined macro or market states.

Section: Derivatives
A derivative derives value from an underlying asset, index, or rate.
Forward contracts are customized OTC agreements to buy or sell in the future.
Futures are standardized exchange traded forwards with daily margining.
Options give the right, not obligation, to buy (call) or sell (put) at a strike price.
Option premium is the upfront price of the option contract.
Intrinsic value is immediate exercise value; time value is the remainder of option price.
Implied volatility is the market implied future volatility embedded in option prices.
Greeks measure option sensitivity: delta, gamma, theta, vega, and rho.
Swaps exchange cash flow streams, such as fixed for floating interest rates.
Hedging uses derivatives to reduce unwanted risk exposures.
Speculation uses derivatives to express directional or volatility views.
Leverage amplifies gains and losses and increases risk of ruin.

Section: Corporate Finance
Corporations finance operations through equity, debt, and retained earnings.
Cost of equity is investor required return for equity risk.
Cost of debt is borrowing rate adjusted for tax shield.
Weighted average cost of capital combines cost of equity and after tax cost of debt.
Capital budgeting evaluates investment projects using NPV, IRR, and payback.
Net present value is present value of cash inflows minus outflows.
Internal rate of return is the discount rate that sets NPV to zero.
Positive NPV projects should create shareholder value if assumptions hold.
Working capital equals current assets minus current liabilities.
Cash conversion cycle measures days inventory plus receivables minus payables days.
Mergers and acquisitions can create synergies but also integration and valuation risk.
Capital structure decisions balance tax benefits of debt against distress risk.

Section: Accounting and Financial Statements
Income statement reports revenue, expenses, and profit over a period.
Balance sheet reports assets, liabilities, and equity at a point in time.
Cash flow statement reports operating, investing, and financing cash movements.
Revenue recognition timing affects earnings quality.
Accrual accounting can diverge from cash generation.
Depreciation allocates cost of tangible assets over useful life.
Amortization allocates cost of intangible assets over useful life.
Gross margin equals gross profit divided by revenue.
Operating margin equals operating income divided by revenue.
Net margin equals net income divided by revenue.
Return on equity equals net income divided by average shareholders equity.
Return on invested capital measures operating profitability relative to invested capital.
Leverage ratios include debt to equity and debt to EBITDA.
Coverage ratios include interest coverage and fixed charge coverage.

Section: Macroeconomics and Policy
Gross domestic product measures aggregate economic output.
Inflation is sustained increase in general price level.
Consumer price index and personal consumption expenditures are common inflation measures.
Unemployment rate measures share of labor force without work but seeking employment.
Central banks set policy rates and manage liquidity to meet inflation and employment mandates.
Monetary tightening raises rates to cool demand and inflation pressure.
Monetary easing lowers rates or expands balance sheet to support growth and credit.
Fiscal policy uses government spending and taxation to influence economic activity.
Business cycles include expansion, peak, contraction, and trough phases.
Leading indicators can signal future economic momentum changes.
Real interest rate equals nominal rate minus expected inflation.
Currency appreciation can reduce export competitiveness but lower import costs.

Section: Market Microstructure and Trading
Bid is highest buyer price; ask is lowest seller price.
Bid ask spread is a key transaction cost and liquidity indicator.
Order book shows resting buy and sell orders at different price levels.
Market orders prioritize execution; limit orders prioritize price.
Slippage is the difference between expected and realized execution price.
Market impact is price movement caused by executing a trade.
Algorithmic execution methods include TWAP, VWAP, and participation strategies.
Settlement is final transfer of cash and securities after trade date.
Counterparty risk is the risk the other party fails to perform.

Section: Personal Finance and Planning
Budgeting aligns spending with income, goals, and constraints.
Emergency funds provide liquidity for unexpected expenses.
Debt management prioritizes high interest debt reduction.
Credit score affects borrowing costs and credit access.
Insurance transfers specific risks in exchange for premiums.
Retirement planning uses expected expenses, longevity, and savings rate assumptions.
Tax planning improves after tax returns through account selection and timing.
Goal based investing aligns portfolios to specific liabilities and timelines.

Section: Behavioral Finance
Loss aversion means losses feel larger than equivalent gains.
Overconfidence can lead to excessive trading and concentration.
Anchoring biases decisions toward initial reference points.
Recency bias overweights recent events and underweights long run evidence.
Herd behavior can amplify bubbles and crashes.
Confirmation bias favors information that supports existing beliefs.
A disciplined process and pre committed rules reduce behavioral errors.

Section: Risk Management and Governance
Risk management identifies, measures, monitors, and mitigates risks.
Major risk types include market, credit, liquidity, operational, legal, and model risk.
Position limits and stop loss policies constrain portfolio level losses.
Independent risk oversight improves governance and accountability.
Model validation and backtesting assess model reliability.
Compliance ensures adherence to laws, regulations, and internal policies.
Know your customer and anti money laundering controls reduce financial crime risk.

Section: ESG and Sustainable Finance
Environmental factors include emissions, energy use, and climate risk exposure.
Social factors include labor standards, community impact, and customer welfare.
Governance factors include board quality, incentives, and shareholder rights.
Sustainable investing approaches include exclusion, integration, thematic, and impact investing.
Green bonds finance projects with environmental objectives.
Transition risk and physical risk are key climate finance dimensions.

Section: Fintech and Digital Finance
Fintech applies technology to payments, lending, wealth management, and insurance.
Digital wallets and real time payments improve transaction speed and user experience.
Open banking enables secure data sharing through APIs with customer consent.
Tokenization represents real world assets as programmable digital tokens.
Smart contracts automate execution based on predefined conditions.
Cybersecurity is essential due to data sensitivity and operational concentration.

Section: Benchmarking and Performance Attribution
A benchmark is a reference portfolio used to evaluate performance.
Absolute return is total return without benchmark comparison.
Relative return is performance versus benchmark.
Attribution decomposes return into allocation, selection, and interaction effects.
Brinson attribution is common for multi asset and equity portfolios.
Fees, taxes, and turnover materially affect net investor outcomes.

Section: Common Learning Map
Beginner topics: budgeting, saving, compounding, diversification, inflation, and basic asset classes.
Intermediate topics: valuation multiples, bond duration, asset allocation, and risk adjusted metrics.
Advanced topics: derivatives Greeks, factor investing, credit analysis, and macro regime allocation.
Professional topics: portfolio construction under constraints, stress testing, and governance frameworks.
